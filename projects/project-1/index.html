    Information about pineapple

Pineapple (scientific name: Ananas comosus) is a species of tropical plant from the bromeliad family that grows in the wild in Brazil, Bolivia and Paraguay. The pineapple is a small perennial plant with 30â€“100 cm long leaves surrounding a thick stem. Each plant produces one fruit that comes off the stem.

    The structure of the pineapple

The skin of the fruit consists of spiky hexagonal protrusions, the outer skin of each hexagon consists of three leaflets under which is a yellow or white membrane. The hexagons are attached to a core attached to the stem of the plant. The size of the pineapple can reach up to 30 cm in length and weigh 1-3 kg. In nature, pineapples contain many seeds, one for each flower. In contrast, commercially grown pineapples almost never contain seeds.

    The uses of the fruit

Pineapple is often used in desserts and fruit salads, but it is also used in many other dishes. A famous example is a Hawaiian-style pizza, which is topped with pineapple and smoked meat. Another well-known use of pineapple is in Asian dishes of noodles in stir-fried sauce with various vegetables (such as cauliflower, broccoli, beans and more). In Hawaii it is customary to use pineapple juice to make an alcoholic drink called "pineapple wine". Because of the special enzymes in it, pineapple juice can be used as a silage or as a meat tenderizer. Also, there are many drinks such as pineapple juice or smoothies in which pineapple is combined.

    processing history

The pineapple spread from its natural growing area under the influence of civilization, and by the time of Christopher Columbus it had reached all over South and Central America and the Caribbean islands. Columbus brought the pineapple with him on his return to Europe, and from Spain he moved to the Philippines and Hawaii. The fruit was successfully acclimatized in greenhouses across Europe as early as 1720. The name of the fruit in the English language (Pineapple) is derived from its pine cone-like appearance.
